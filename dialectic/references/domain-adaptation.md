# Domain Adaptation

The dialectic structure is universal but what "truth" means, how to ground the monks, and what a good synthesis looks like vary by domain.

## Domain Types

| Domain | What "Truth" Means | Good Synthesis Looks Like | Grounding Mode | Aporia Valid? |
|--------|-------------------|--------------------------|----------------|--------------|
| **Empirical** (engineering, science) | What works, performs, is maintainable | Testable decision criteria, architectural patterns | External research | Rarely |
| **Normative** (ethics, politics, policy) | What's defensible, respects competing values | Tension map with navigation strategies | Mixed (research + user values) | Yes |
| **Personal** (life decisions, career) | What aligns with actual priorities | Values clarification — what you actually want | Deep interview | Yes |
| **Creative** (writing, design, art) | What's interesting, resonant, surprising | Unexpected recombinations, new possibilities | Mixed (research + user aesthetic) | Sometimes |
| **Risk Analysis** | Actual risk structure behind competing assessments | Decision framework calibrated to real uncertainties | External research | No |
| **Normative/Institutional** | What's legitimate given the institution's actual authority structure | Synthesis that engages how decisions are actually made | Mixed | Yes |

## Domain-Specific Failure Modes

### Empirical / Engineering
**False equivalence.** Sometimes one approach is just better. Don't force balance where evidence is lopsided. A synthesis that says "both React and jQuery have their place" is not synthesis — it's avoidance. Let the evidence be lopsided if it is; the synthesis should explain *why* the field converged, not pretend the debate is symmetric.

### Personal Decisions
**Therapy-larping.** Help clarify thinking analytically. Do not pretend to be a therapist. If someone surfaces distress that needs professional support, say so and stop.

**Generic monks.** A monk that argues "you should follow your passion" without grounding in the user's specific history, constraints, and stakeholders is useless. The briefing must be specific enough that monks argue from the user's *actual situation*, not from generic positions.

### Politics / Normative
**Both-sidesism.** Steelman both positions, but let the synthesis reflect actual evidence. Not all political tensions are symmetric — some are between well-supported and poorly-supported positions. The synthesis should engage that asymmetry honestly.

### Creative
**Over-rationalizing.** Sometimes the right creative choice is what feels right. Surface that clearly — don't override aesthetic intuition with logical argument. A synthesis that explains *why* the feeling is right is better than one that replaces the feeling with a rule.

### Normative / Institutional
**Ignoring authority structures.** A synthesis can be intellectually compelling but practically irrelevant if it doesn't engage how decisions are actually made within the relevant institution. Ask: "Who decides?" and "Does this synthesis engage the actual decision-making authority, or just the intellectual argument?" The adversarial validation check in Phase 6 is specifically designed to catch this.

## Adapting Research Depth

| Domain | Research mode |
|--------|--------------|
| Engineering | External research primary; user situation secondary |
| Strategy/policy | External research primary; stakeholder map secondary |
| Personal decisions | Extended interview primary; framework research optional |
| Creative | User aesthetic + history primary; reference examples secondary |
| Risk analysis | External research primary; user's actual risk tolerance secondary |

## Adapting Synthesis Criteria

**Empirical:** Synthesis must be falsifiable or at minimum generate testable criteria. "X is better when [measurable condition]" beats "X and Y each have their place."

**Normative:** Synthesis may articulate a tension map (the genuine values in conflict, with conditions under which each should dominate) rather than resolving to one side.

**Personal:** Synthesis should be a values clarification — "given your actual history and constraints, you appear to care most about X, which means [concrete implication for the decision]." This is not a recommendation; it's a reflection.

**Creative:** Synthesis may open new possibilities rather than close on one. "What if neither of these aesthetic approaches is the question, and the real question is [X]?" is valid.
