# Worked Examples

Three annotated examples showing what good execution looks like at each level of difficulty.

---

## Example 1: TanStack Start vs. Next.js (Technical Architecture)

**Surface framing:** "Should I use TanStack Start or Next.js?"

**Degenerate framing to avoid:** "Libraries vs. frameworks" or "modular vs. monolithic." Both are opinionated frameworks. This isn't the real contradiction.

**Deepest contradiction found:** Infrastructure sovereignty and incentive alignment (open infrastructure primitives, community-governed) vs. deep framework-infrastructure integration commercially sustained by Vercel's business model.

### Framing Correction (Monk A)
> "TanStack Start IS a framework — it has opinions about routing, server functions, SSR, and application architecture. Your argument is NOT that TanStack Start is more modular while Next.js is a monolith. The real difference lies in the relationship between the framework, the infrastructure it targets, and the business interests that fund its development."

### Framing Correction (Monk B)
> "Your opponent's argument is NOT the naive 'vendor lock-in' complaint. They will argue that Next.js's architecture is structurally compromised by Vercel's commercial interests at the design level. You need to engage this argument directly, not dismiss it as a hosting preference."

### Research Directives (Monk A)
1. Search for Vinxi and Nitro as open infrastructure primitives
2. Search for structural arguments about how Vercel's business model shapes Next.js architectural decisions — not superficial lock-in complaints

### Research Directives (Monk B)
1. Search for React core team arguments for Server Components and the distinction between RSC and Next.js's implementation
2. Search for concrete evidence of Next.js deployment capabilities outside Vercel

### Ontological Question Driving Both
"What is the proper relationship between a framework, the infrastructure it runs on, and the business interests that fund its development?"

### Key Lesson
The Phase 1 framing correction was the highest-value work. Without it, both monks would have produced the boring "modularity vs. batteries-included" dialectic that everyone already knows. The correction forced both into the structural question about incentive alignment — which is the actual tension for teams making long-term architectural decisions.

## Example 2: Career vs. Family Commitment (Personal Values)

**Surface framing:** "I'm torn between taking this promotion and being more present for my kids."

**Degenerate framing to avoid:** "Work-life balance." This flattens a structural tension into a scheduling problem.

**Deepest contradiction found (via extended interview):** The user doesn't just want both — they believe that *being the kind of person* who excels at work is inseparable from *being the kind of parent* they want to be. The tension is identity-level, not time-allocation.

**No external research needed.** The context briefing was built entirely from the elenctic interview.

### Framing Correction (Monk A)
> "Your argument is NOT that career success matters. It's that THIS USER'S specific professional identity — what they build, how they lead, what they model for their children — is itself an act of parenting. Ground this in their actual history: [specific examples from interview]. The promotion represents not just income or status but a form of self-expression that the user believes shapes who their children become."

### Framing Correction (Monk B)
> "Your argument is NOT that family time matters. It's that presence has a developmental window that closes — and that the user's children at ages [X] need [specific things surfaced in interview] that no amount of 'quality time' can compress into fewer hours. Ground this in the specific stakeholder map: partner's actual capacity, what the children's schedules actually look like."

### Key Lessons
1. **Specificity is everything in personal domains.** A generic monk ("follow your passion") is useless. A monk that argues from the user's actual commitment history, their children's developmental stage, their partner's real capacity — that's an Electric Monk doing its job.
2. **The identity-level reframe is the deepest available tension.** The surface scheduling conflict dissolves once you see it as an identity coherence problem. This is what the elenctic probing was designed to surface.
3. **Aporia is sometimes the right output.** This dialectic may end with "here is the structural tension you're actually in, and here is what each resolution costs at the identity level" — not a recommendation. That's more valuable than a confident answer.

## Example 3: Agent Identity and Governance (7-Cycle Recursive)

This example shows how recursion pulls in cross-domain material at each cycle — Boyd's prediction made concrete.

| Round | Contradiction | Synthesis | New Material Pulled In |
|-------|--------------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | "Is the agent the code or the pattern?" | Agent as resonance pattern | Stream theory |
| 2 | "Where does identity live?" | Address identity vs. semantic identity | Naming/identity theory |
| 3 | "Can a grammar be both simple and expressive?" | Metacognition decomposition | Cognitive science |
| 4 | "Who governs the governors?" | Jurisprudential streams | Legal theory, constitutional design, Gödel's incompleteness |
| 5 | "Should the SDK look familiar or teach new concepts?" | Simple API surface, rich feedback surface | Pedagogical theory |
| 6 | "Can agents be tested through their streams alone?" | Witness obligations | Evidentiary standards, cryptographic verification |
| 7 | "Must the verification chain terminate in trust?" | The Incompleteness Engine | Gödel again, N-of-M quorum systems, adversarial red-teaming |

The original question has nothing to do with jurisprudence or Gödel — but by Cycle 4, those concepts were essential. Each synthesis opened doors to domains the previous round couldn't see. This is exactly what Boyd predicts: the destructive step liberates parts that can now connect with material from outside the original domains.

### Key Lessons from the 7-Cycle Run
1. **The most valuable insight came in Round 4.** It couldn't have been reached without Rounds 1–3 building the conceptual vocabulary.
2. **The hostile auditor in Round 3 identified the recursion direction for Round 4.** Its "harder contradiction" — "who governs the governors?" — was better than what the orchestrator had identified.
3. **Fresh agents outperformed resumed sessions from Round 3 onward.** By then the conceptual space had evolved enough that carrying prior conviction was more hindrance than help.
4. **The queue became a map.** By Round 5, the user could see the shape of the intellectual territory — which questions had been resolved, which remained open, which were becoming less productive. That map was itself a deliverable.

## What Makes Good Prompts Work

Across all three examples, the same elements separate good prompts from generic ones:

1. **Targeted research directives.** Not "research TanStack Start" but "search for the structural argument that Vercel's business model shapes Next.js at the design level." Grounds the monk in real detail.
2. **Framing corrections preempt degenerate dialectics.** Without them, monks default to the boring, obvious take.
3. **Ontological questions force depth.** "What IS the proper relationship between X and Y?" pushes past feature comparison into structural argument.
4. **"Push to the extreme" with explicit permission.** Counters RLHF agreeableness.
5. **Opponent restatement prevents shadowboxing.** "Your opponent's argument is NOT the naive take — they will argue [ACTUAL ARGUMENT]."
6. **Personal domains ground in specifics.** The interview must produce enough material that monks argue from the user's actual situation.
7. **Parallel structure enables comparison.** Both prompts follow the same shape so outputs are structurally comparable for Phase 4.
